Patterns of Transit Accessibility in Berkeley

An analysis on how transit accessibility in Berkeley varies across space and time.

Introduction

The City of Berkeley is home to over 120,000 residents 1 , including families and students, spread across 10.5 square miles 2 . With roughly 12,000 residents per square mile, Berkeley is among the most densely populated cities in California.

In such environments, uneven transit access can create meaningful constraints on daily mobility particularly if areas with lower service availability coincide with households that are less likely to own a car. Understanding whether transit accessibility is distributed consistently across space and time is therefore important for evaluating how well the transit system aligns with underlying mobility needs.

This raises an important question: within Berkeley, how does spatial transit accessibility—defined as coverage within a 6-minute (500 m) walk of an AC Transit stop—and temporal transit accessibility—defined as weekday scheduled bus arrivals per 1,000 residents by time block—relate to 2020 vehicle ownership patterns across census tracts?

Data Description

Population data is measured at the census block level, while vehicle ownership data is available at the census tract level. Spatial boundary files define the study area and support alignment across census geographies. Transit data include both bus stop locations and scheduled weekday service by stop, representing planned service availability.

Data Preperation

Minor data cleaning was performed to ensure consistency across datasets. All spatial layers were projected to a common coordinate reference system and filtered to Berkeley census tracts. No imputation was required for missing census attributes.

Methods

The analysis is restricted to the Berkeley land boundary to exclude non-residential areas without population, pedestrian networks, or bus stops. Census tracts, census blocks, and bus stops were filtered to this boundary.

Walking access is defined as a 6-minute (approximately 500 m) network-based walk from a bus stop. 3 Isochrones for each stop were generated using an OSMnx pedestrian network of Berkeley and unioned into a single polygon representing walkable access. Population coverage was measured at the census block level using area-weighted intersection with the walk access polygon. Walking access was visualized using an interactive map, where individual layers can be toggled on and off.

Transit access is defined as scheduled AC Transit bus arrivals. Hourly weekday schedules were obtained by querying the AC Transit API for all unique bus routes, then aggregated into time blocks and assigned to census tracts based on stop location. Bus arrivals were then normalized by the population covered within each tract. Transit access was visualized using a spatiotemporal choropleth map and box plots to summarize variation in transit accessibility across census tracts and time periods.

Building on these visualizations, box plots were used to examine the distribution of transit accessibility across time blocks and to identify periods with greater variability for focused analysis. Census tracts were then classified based on whether their accessibility values fell in the bottom 25% (low-access) or top 25% (high-access) citywide within the selected time block. These groups were compared to assess differences of zero-vehicle households across levels of transit access as part of equity analysis.

Equity is defined as how evenly transit access is distributed across residents and evaluated by comparing zero-vehicle household shares between lower-access (bottom 25%) and higher-access (top 25%) census tracts within each time block.

Results

Approximately 115,890 residents (93.2%) of Berkeley’s population live within a 6-minute walk of at least one AC Transit bus stop, indicating near-universal spatial coverage. However, a small share of residents remains outside walkable transit catchments.

Citywide weekday scheduled arrivals per 1,000 residents vary substantially by time of day and peaking during the midday period, even when restricting the analysis to residents within walking distance of a bus stop.

Two census tracts corresponding to the Berkeley Marina and the UC Berkeley campus were excluded from choropleth visualizations due to very low residential populations that inflate per-capita values. These tracts are dominated by non-residential land uses and are not comparable to surrounding neighborhoods.

Although median bus arrivals are highest during midday, the interquartile range and whiskers are widest during this period, suggesting greater spatial unevenness despite higher overall service levels, motivating further analysis. Census tracts were classified based on whether their midday accessibility fell in the bottom 25% or top 75% percentiles. Comparisons of zero-vehicle household shares across these groups show similar medians and substantial overlap. After excluding a campus-adjacent outlier tract dominated by the student population, differences between groups are minimal.

Limitations

Our analysis, while informative, contains limitations. For example, we measure scheduled transit accessibility rather than real-time reliability, delays, or service disruptions. Furthermore, walking access thresholds do not account for differences in mobility, pedestrian conditions, or terrain. Tracts that have very small residential populations relative to transit service levels inflated transit access values driven by small denominators. These tracts were excluded from some visualizations to avoid misleading comparisons. While the 2020 Census may reflect pandemic-related distortions, we used it to locate residential populations relative to infrastructure rather than to model travel behavior or transit demand. Overall, while there are factors that may obscure how transit accessibility is experienced in practice, the analysis provides a structured view of spatial and temporal transit accessibility.

Conclusion

Within Berkeley, where most residents live within walking distance of transit (93.2%), variation in scheduled bus service frequency does not show a strong relationship with vehicle ownership at the census tract level. This result does not imply that transit frequency has no effect on travel behavior, but rather that tract-level differences in frequency are not associated with differences in car ownership. From a policy perspective, this suggests that service frequency alone may be an incomplete indicator of transit equity, at least during the midday period when frequency and spatial variance was observed to be highest.
